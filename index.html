Part one · The chart note

Two AI Models, One Trendline: How Long Does the Discount Last?

Bitcoin topped near $126K in October 2025. Since then it's fallen again and again. On June 10, 2026, we printed a fresh low around $61,500. That's half the paper value gone in eight months.

So what does that mean for the Power Law trendline? Two AI models, Claude and ChatGPT, got the same question independently. Same data, same starting point, no peeking at each other's homework: we're way below the trendline right now. If the line holds, when do we get back to it?

Before the answers, here's the thing. This is technically a price model. But that's not what it's for.

What it really measures is time.

How long does Bitcoin stay below its trendline, and what does that window mean if you're still stacking? If you believe the line holds, time below it isn't bad news. It's the sale. The model just tells you how long the sale might run.

That reframe changes how you read everything below.

Where they agreed

Almost everywhere, honestly.

  • Same anchor. Both started at $61,500, a spot price from June 2026.
  • Same trendline. Both put the Power Law 1.0x line near $133K at the time.
  • Same multiplier. Spot's sitting at 0.46x of trend. Deep below the line.
  • Same verdict. The Power Law didn't break. The old assumption that we'd be back on the line by mid-2028 did. Both models said the same thing: keep the equation, re-anchor to the current spot, move the dates.
  • Same worst case. Both landed on the exact same slow-recovery date, December 31, 2030. Not close. The same day.

That last one got me. Two different models, built by two different companies, looked at the same hole and picked the same bottom of the range.

And that 0.46x number deserves a minute of your time. The last time Bitcoin spent real time below half its trendline was the 2022 bottom. Those months turned out to be the best buying windows on record. Nobody enjoyed them while they were happening. And that's what's great about opportunities like this.

Where they split

One difference showed up everywhere. ChatGPT put every recovery date about six months later than Claude.

Same scenarios, two clocks:

  • Bull case: Claude says December 2027, ChatGPT says June 2028.
  • Base case: Claude says December 2028, ChatGPT says June 2029.
  • Bear case: both say December 2030.

Here's the part I keep coming back to. June 2028 used to be an unremarkable base case for getting back to trend. After this drawdown, ChatGPT moved it. June 2028 is now its best case, not its middle one. Claude kept a faster line above it. That one shift is the whole disagreement.

Lay the dates in order and you get a clean six-month staircase. December 2027, June 2028, December 2028, June 2029. Then a gap, and both models holding hands on the December 2030 bear.

How realistic are the fast ones? Claude's December 2027 needs about 123% annualized growth from here. ChatGPT's June 2028 needs about 97%. Sounds impossible until you check the last recovery. November 2022 to March 2024 was a 4.7x move in sixteen months, north of 200% annualized. Both fast lines are slower than what actually happened two years ago.

The catch: that run launched off a confirmed bottom. We don't have one of those. We've got a fresh low from June 2026.

Now look at what even the most bullish line admits. About eighteen months below the trendline. The single most optimistic number either model produced still leaves a year and a half of discounted stacking. They're arguing about how long the window stays open. Neither one is arguing about whether it's open.

The price range, since you're going to ask

Take the most bullish line either model drew and the most bearish:

  • Summer 2026: both edges sit between $63K and $69K. The disagreement barely exists yet.
  • Mid 2027: $102K on the slow line, $144K on the fast one.
  • January 2029: $205K slow, $287K fast.

That near-term agreement is what makes this thing usable. You don't have to know which 2029 is coming to know what to do in 2026.

What could break it

Both models assumed the catch-up happens at all. The real tail risk lives outside this whole exercise, in the world where the Power Law just stops describing Bitcoin.

Eight months of decline isn't evidence of that. A 51% drawdown at 0.46x is well inside fifteen years of normal behavior for this thing. But I'll tell you my own tripwire: if we're still carving lower lows deep into 2027 and the multiplier is pressing toward 0.35x, the question changes from "when do we catch up" to "does the line still hold."

Until then, I watch the multiplier, not the price. The price is noise. The distance from trend is the signal.

So here's where I land, at the end of a rough week in a rough year.

The fire sale is short. The discount is long. And the slowest recovery is the cheapest stack.

If you believe, this isn't the season to doom-scroll. It's the season to put in the proof of work that finds you more fiat to stack while the multiplier is this low.

DCA 110: The Longest Sale Buys the Most Sats

Part one ended with one line: the fire sale is short, the discount is long, and the slowest recovery is the cheapest stack. This part is the receipts.

Here's the game. Starting July 1, 2026, you add an extra $110 to whatever you already stack. A month, a week, a day — your call. And you keep that extra going until spot climbs back to the Power Law trendline. Two AI models drew four glide paths back to the line, so there are four ending dates: December 2027, December 2028, June 2029, and December 2030.

How the math works

  • Both AI models anchored at June 10, 2026: spot $61.5K, trendline $133K, a 0.46x multiplier.
  • Each scenario glides that multiplier smoothly back to 1.0x by its catch-up date. Spot on any day is the multiplier times the trendline.
  • Your extra $110 buys at that day's spot price — monthly on the 1st, weekly every seven days, daily every day — starting July 1, 2026, and ending the day the line touches trend.
  • On day one, July 1, 2026, that spot price sits around $63–64K depending on the scenario, since each glide path has climbed a little off the June 10 anchor at its own pace, against a July trendline of $135.5K.
  • The stack's final value is your Bitcoin times the trendline price on the catch-up date.

Notice what the four cards have in common: the bear case takes in the most dollars, because the sale runs longest. That's not a trick. That's the whole point. More months under the line means more chances to buy under the line, and every one of those buys is valued at the line when the catch-up finally comes.

And the multiple is biggest in the bear case too, because the trendline itself keeps climbing for four and a half years while you're stacking under it. The longest wait pays the most per dollar and takes the most dollars. Proof of work, in fiat form.

What this isn't

A prediction. Real prices will chop above and below every line here, and your real buys will land all over that chop. This is a deterministic toy built on smooth glide paths, so the numbers are exact for the model and approximate for reality. The model can also just be wrong — part one covers what would break it.

It's also not a dare to stack more than you can. The tool works the same at $110 a month or $20 a month. The point isn't the size of the number, it's seeing what time under the line does when the number is honestly yours.

Every Bitcoiner can look back at a month they wish they could have bought again. This page is just the math for the next one.
