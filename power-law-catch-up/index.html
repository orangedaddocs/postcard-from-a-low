Two AI Models, One Trendline: How Long Does the Discount Last?

Bitcoin topped near $126K in October 2025. Since then it's fallen over and over. On June 10, 2026, we printed a fresh low around $61,500. That's half the paper value gone in eight months.

So what does that mean for the Power Law trendline? Two AI models, Claude and ChatGPT, got the same question independently. Same data, same starting point, no peeking at each other's homework: we're way below the trendline right now. If the line holds, when do we get back to it?

Before the answers, here's the thing. This is technically a price model. But that's not what it's for.

What it really measures is time.

How long does Bitcoin stay below its trendline, and what does that window mean if you're still stacking? If you believe the line holds, time below it isn't bad news. It's the sale. The model just tells you how long the sale might run.

That reframe changes how you read everything below.

Where they agreed

Almost everywhere, honestly.

  • Same anchor. Both started at $61,500, a spot price from June 2026.
  • Same trendline. Both put the Power Law 1.0x line near $133K at the time.
  • Same multiplier. Spot's sitting at 0.46x of trend. Deep below the line.
  • Same verdict. The Power Law didn't break. The old assumption that we'd be back on the line by mid-2028 did. Both models said the same thing: keep the equation, re-anchor to the current spot, move the dates.
  • Same worst case. Both landed on the exact same slow-recovery date, December 31, 2030. Not close. The same day.

That last one got me. Two different models, built by two different companies, looked at the same hole and picked the same bottom of the range.

And that 0.46x number deserves a minute of your time. The last time Bitcoin spent real time below half its trendline was the 2022 bottom. Those months turned out to be the best buying windows on record. Nobody enjoyed them while they were happening. And that's what's great about opportunities like this.

Where they split

One difference, and it showed up everywhere: ChatGPT pushed every recovery date about six months later than Claude.

  • Claude: back to trend by December 2027 in the bull case, December 2028 in the base case.
  • ChatGPT: June 2028 bull, June 2029 base.

Here's the part I keep coming back to. Before this drawdown, June 2028 looked like a perfectly reasonable base case for getting back to trend. ChatGPT looked at the new hole and demoted that date from expected to optimistic. Claude kept a faster line above it. Lay all five dates out and you get a clean six-month staircase: December 2027, June 2028, December 2028, June 2029, and then both models in agreement on the December 2030 bear.

How realistic are the fast ones? Claude's December 2027 line needs roughly 123% annualized growth from here. ChatGPT's June 2028 line needs about 97%. Sounds crazy until you remember the last recovery ran hotter than both. November 2022 to March 2024 was a 4.7x move in sixteen months, north of 200% annualized.

The catch: that move launched off a confirmed bottom. We don't have one of those. We have a fresh low from June 2026. One model bet on the bounce. The other bet on the wait.

And look at what even the most bullish line in the room admits: about eighteen months below the trendline. The single most optimistic scenario either model produced still leaves a year and a half of discounted stacking. They're arguing about how long the window stays open. Neither one is arguing about whether it exists.

The discount window, measured

This is where the reframe pays off. Run every scenario from both models and ask one question: how long does spot stay in each zone below the trendline?

The fire-sale zone, below 0.5x of trend. That's where we are right now.

  • Fastest scenario: we're out by August.
  • Slowest: out by late November.

The deep-discount zone, below 0.75x.

  • Fastest: lasts until June 2027. About a year.
  • Middle: early-to-mid 2028.
  • Slowest: April 2029. Almost three years.

Below the trendline, period.

  • Fastest: 19 months.
  • Middle: 31 to 37 months.
  • Slowest: 55 months. Four and a half years of buying under the line.

The "bear case" in this model isn't a crash. It's the longest sale. If you're stacking on a schedule, the scenario that looks worst on a price chart is the one that hands you the most Bitcoin.

What could break it

Both models assumed the catch-up happens at all. The real tail risk lives outside this whole exercise, in the world where the Power Law just stops describing Bitcoin.

Eight months of decline isn't evidence of that. A 51% drawdown at 0.46x is well inside fifteen years of normal behavior for this thing. But I'll tell you my own tripwire: if we're still carving lower lows deep into 2027 and the multiplier is pressing toward 0.35x, the question changes from "when do we catch up" to "does the line still hold."

Until then, I watch the multiplier, not the price. The price is noise. The distance from trend is the signal.

So here's where I land, at the end of a rough week in a rough year.

The fire sale is short. The discount is long. And the slowest recovery is the cheapest stack.

If you believe, this isn't the season to doom-scroll. It's the season to put in the proof of work that finds you more fiat to stack while the multiplier is this low.
